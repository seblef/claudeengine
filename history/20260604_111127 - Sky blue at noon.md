# Sky blue at noon — issue #380

**Branch**: `fix/sky-yellow-noon-380`
**Issue**: #380
**Date**: 2026-06-04

---

## Problem

At noon the sun disc and halo used the same warm orange-yellow colours
regardless of sun elevation:

```glsl
sky_rgb += vec3(60.0, 48.0, 24.0) * sun_disc * sun_vis;  // ratio 5:4:2 — warm
sky_rgb += vec3(2.0,  1.6,  0.9)  * sun_halo * sun_vis;  // warm
```

The Preetham sky background is correctly blue at noon (zenith chromaticity
x≈0.241, y≈0.244 for T=2, θ_s=45°), but the warm halo extends ~8° around
the sun disc and the strong Preetham forward-scattering peak (C_Y ≈ 5.28 at
T=2) amplifies brightness near the sun direction. The combined warm disc +
halo + forward-scatter peak made the sky near the sun appear yellow/orange.

At noon the sun is at 45° elevation, which is high in the sky — a prominent
position where the warm area is easily noticed against the blue background.

## Root cause

Physically, sunlight appears orange-red at sunrise/sunset because the longer
slant atmospheric path scatters blue wavelengths away (Rayleigh scattering).
At noon the path is short and the sun appears near-white. The previous
implementation used fixed warm colours regardless of elevation, which was only
accurate at the horizon.

## Fix

**`data/shaders/glsl/sky/sky_ps.glsl`**

Replaced the fixed warm disc and halo colours with elevation-dependent blends:

```glsl
// t transitions 0→1 over the first 15° of elevation (sin 15° ≈ 0.259).
float sun_elev_t = clamp(sun_direction.y / 0.259, 0.0, 1.0);
sky_rgb += mix(vec3(60.0, 36.0, 10.0), vec3(60.0, 58.0, 54.0),
               sun_elev_t) * sun_disc * sun_vis;
sky_rgb += mix(vec3(2.0, 1.0, 0.3), vec3(1.6, 1.5, 1.4),
               sun_elev_t) * sun_halo * sun_vis;
```

- **Sunrise/sunset** (elevation < 15°): deep orange-red disc `(60, 36, 10)`,
  warm halo `(2.0, 1.0, 0.3)`
- **Noon** (elevation ≥ 15°): near-white disc `(60, 58, 54)`,
  neutral halo `(1.6, 1.5, 1.4)`
- The disc values all clip to white after gamma (1/2.2), so the disc itself
  always appears white; only the halo region carries the tint into visible range.

The 15° threshold matches the conventional "civil twilight" boundary below
which the slant path is long enough for significant chromatic attenuation.

## Output to keep in mind

- The Preetham model itself (`PreethamSky`) is unchanged — it is physically
  correct. The yellow sky was purely due to the sun disc/halo colour choices.
- The sky at zenith at noon has chromaticity (x≈0.241, y≈0.244) which is in
  the sky-blue region of the CIE 1931 diagram — always was, even before this fix.
- `sun_elev_t` uses `sun_direction.y / 0.259` (not sun_vis or day_factor) so
  it correctly tracks actual elevation independent of the day/night transition.
- Turbidity default (2.0) and `kLuminanceScale` (0.05) are unchanged.
